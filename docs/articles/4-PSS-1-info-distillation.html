Allow Me to Service this Questionnaire Professionally #1 - Information Distillation

Uploaded on 2025 June 7th

I've been wanting to write an article with this title for a while - mostly because Information Distillation sounds cool, I admit. It also sounds arcane a bit, like I'm an alchemist trying to create a Philosopher's Stone which I will use along with a couple dozen brains to create the Juice of KnowledgeTM. Yum.

The first part, the one that makes this sound like this article is the first in a series of articles comes from the fact that at the moment I'm part of the "Professional Services" team at my company. When I get to take my first look at a questionnaire, it's already been deemed "Ready for Professional Services" and assigned to me. So because I'm approx. five years old, I always think:

That is me.
I am Professional Services.
Allow me to service this questionnaire professionally.

Hehehe

Now, this article is supposed to be about Information Distillation - what do I mean by that? I mean that in the above-mentioned questionnaires I frequently encounter situations or instructions that seem a bit overcomplicated, too wordy. Sometimes it's justifiably complicated, because it's describing a complicated situation. Other times I get the feeling that multiple people worked on the same questionnaire, but not necessarily at the same time or with the same information, or with not enough time to get to know the questionnaire.

To better illustrate what I mean, you can find a short excerpt from an imaginary study below.

  • ...

  • QGEN. What do you identify as?
    Select one

  • QCHILD. Do you have children?
    Select one

  • ...

What happened in this imaginary study is that new gender options kept being added, and they kept being added to the instructions at QCHILD as well. You can note that the above instructions are still correct, but they can be significantly shortened if we switch to a positive affirmation:
